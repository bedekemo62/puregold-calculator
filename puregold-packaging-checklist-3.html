Introduction

Why Most Businesses Waste Money on Packaging

After 20 years of working with product manufacturers, SMEs, importers, and emerging brands across West and East Africa, we've seen the same mistakes over and over again.

A business invests in design, orders a print run of 500 or 1,000 pieces — and then discovers the barcode doesn't scan, the label peels off in humidity, the box doesn't fit the product, or the print looks nothing like what was approved.

The result? Wasted money. Delayed launches. Lost customers. Damaged brand reputation.

This checklist exists to make sure that never happens to you. Go through every section before you send anything to print. It will save you more than just money.

"Your packaging is your brand's first handshake with your customer. Get it wrong once and you may never get a second chance."

This guide covers 6 key areas every product brand must check before going to print. Whether you're ordering 100 or 100,000 pieces — these principles apply.

Section 01

Design & Artwork Readiness

Your artwork file must be print-ready before anything goes to production. A beautiful design on screen can look completely different in print if it's not prepared correctly.

  • File is in CMYK colour mode (not RGB) RGB is for screens. CMYK is for print. Sending an RGB file will cause significant colour shifts — especially with greens, blues, and reds.
  • Resolution is at least 300 DPI Low resolution files (72 or 96 DPI) will print blurry. Always export at 300 DPI minimum for sharp, professional results.
  • Bleed area has been added (minimum 3mm) Bleed prevents white edges from appearing after cutting. Any colour or image that runs to the edge of the packaging must extend 3mm beyond the trim line.
  • All fonts are embedded or converted to outlines If your printer doesn't have your font installed, the text will change or disappear. Always outline or embed fonts before sending files.
  • File format is PDF, AI, or EPS (not Word or PNG) Professional print files should be vector-based or high-resolution. Never send a Word document or low-resolution PNG for production work.
  • All text is within the safe zone (at least 5mm from trim edge) Text too close to the edge risks being cut off during trimming. Keep all important text and logos within the safe zone.

Always request a digital proof from your printer before production begins. A good printer will flag artwork issues before they become expensive mistakes.

Never approve a print job based on how it looks on your phone or laptop screen. Colours on screens are brighter and more saturated than what prints on paper or packaging material.

Section 02

Brand Identity Consistency

Your packaging must look and feel like your brand — every time, across every product. Inconsistent branding is one of the fastest ways to lose customer trust.

  • Logo is the correct version (not stretched or distorted) Always use your official logo file. Never stretch, squish, recolour, or recreate your logo from memory.
  • Brand colours are specified as exact CMYK values Saying "dark green" is not enough. Every brand colour must have a specific CMYK value to ensure consistency across all print runs.
  • Typography matches your brand guidelines Use only your brand's official fonts. Mixing random fonts across packaging materials weakens your brand identity.
  • Packaging design is consistent with your other materials Your boxes, labels, bags, and inserts should all look like they belong to the same brand family.
  • Logo is clearly visible and readable at final print size Test your logo at the actual size it will appear on the packaging. Very small logos can become unreadable or lose detail when printed.
"A brand that looks different every time it shows up doesn't look like a brand at all — it looks like a mistake."
Section 03

Packaging Dimensions & Fit

One of the most expensive mistakes in packaging is ordering boxes or labels that don't fit your product. Always verify dimensions before production.

  • Box dimensions have been physically measured against the product Measure your product (length × width × height) and add appropriate clearance. The box should fit the product snugly — not too tight, not too loose.
  • A physical sample or mock-up has been approved before bulk production Always request a sample before committing to a full print run. This is non-negotiable for boxes, cartons, and pouches.
  • Label dimensions match the surface area of the product container A label too large will wrap awkwardly. A label too small will look lost. Measure the actual surface the label will sit on.
  • Bag or pouch size accounts for product weight and volume Pouches and nylon bags must accommodate the product with enough space to seal properly at the top.
  • Dieline / structural template has been reviewed and approved For boxes and cartons, a dieline (flat layout template) shows exactly how the packaging folds. Always review and approve this before print.

When in doubt, always go slightly larger on box dimensions. A product that rattles inside its packaging looks unprofessional. A tight, well-fitted box signals quality and care.

Section 04

Required Product Information

Depending on your product type and target market, your packaging may be legally required to include specific information. Missing these can get your products held at customs or rejected by retailers.

  • Product name and description is clearly displayed Customers and regulators must be able to identify exactly what the product is from the packaging alone.
  • Manufacturer name and contact information is included Required for most consumer products in Nigeria, Kenya, Uganda, and across West/East Africa.
  • Net weight or volume is stated Food products, cosmetics, and beverages must state the quantity of contents clearly on the packaging.
  • Ingredients or composition list is included (where applicable) Mandatory for food, cosmetics, and herbal products. Must be legible — never use a font size below 7pt for ingredient lists.
  • Expiry date / manufacturing date placement is clearly marked Even if you're printing the date later (via hot stamping or inkjet), make sure there is a clearly designated space for it on the packaging.
  • Barcode or QR code is included and has been tested If your product will be sold in supermarkets or retail stores, a working barcode is mandatory. Test it with a scanner before going to print.
  • Regulatory body approvals are displayed (NAFDAC, KEBS, etc.) For regulated products (food, cosmetics, pharmaceuticals), display your regulatory approval number clearly on the packaging.

Packaging that does not meet regulatory requirements can result in product seizure, fines, or a complete recall. Always verify your market's labelling laws before going to print — especially when exporting across borders.

Section 05

Material & Finish Selection

The material your packaging is printed on affects durability, appearance, and cost. Choosing the wrong material for your product or market can be a costly mistake.

Material Best For Not Ideal For
Art Paper / Gloss Paper Labels, flyers, product inserts Humid environments, wet products
Kraft Paper Eco-friendly packaging, paper bags, natural brands Products needing very sharp colour reproduction
Corrugated Board Export cartons, heavy products, shipping boxes Lightweight retail display packaging
BOPP / Waterproof Label Soap, cosmetics, beverages, anything exposed to moisture Dry products stored in low-humidity environments only
Nylon / Polyethylene Food packaging, sachets, bread bags, rice bags Products needing rigid structure
  • Material is appropriate for the product's environment Will the packaging be exposed to heat, moisture, or rough handling? Choose accordingly.
  • Finish has been selected (matte, gloss, soft-touch, UV spot) Matte feels premium and reduces glare. Gloss is vibrant and eye-catching. UV spot adds luxury highlights to specific elements.
  • Material is food-safe (if applicable) Any packaging in direct contact with food must be food-grade certified. Never assume — always confirm with your printer.

Matte lamination is currently the most popular finish among premium African consumer brands. It photographs beautifully, feels expensive in the hand, and ages better than gloss over time.

Section 06

Before You Place Your Order

Your final checklist before sending anything to production. Do not skip these steps — they are the difference between a smooth order and an expensive disappointment.

  • You have received and approved a digital proof Never allow production to begin without approving a digital proof. Once printing starts, changes are costly or impossible.
  • Quantity has been confirmed and aligns with your production needs Order enough to cover your production run plus a small buffer (5–10%) for wastage, sampling, and unexpected demand.
  • Delivery timeline has been confirmed and fits your schedule Always build in buffer time. If you need packaging by a specific date, add at least one week to the agreed delivery date as your personal deadline.
  • Shipping destination and customs requirements have been discussed Especially important for cross-border orders (Nigeria to Kenya, Uganda, Ghana etc.). Confirm all customs documentation in advance.
  • Payment terms and production timeline are clearly agreed in writing Always get your order confirmation, pricing, and timeline in writing before paying. This protects both you and your printer.
  • You have a sample or reference piece for quality comparison If you've worked with a printer before, provide a reference sample from your previous order so they can match the quality standard.

The best time to build a relationship with a reliable printing partner is before you urgently need one. A printer that knows your brand and standards will consistently deliver better results than starting fresh every time.
